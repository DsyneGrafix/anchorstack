The Book of Leviticus: Holiness, Sacrifice, and Divine Law

Leviticus, the third book of the Hebrew Bible and Christian Old Testament, stands as the most detailed manual of worship, sacrifice, and holy living in Scripture. Named after the Levitical priesthood that administered its laws, this book contains the divine instructions given to Moses at Mount Sinai for establishing proper worship and creating a holy nation. Though often considered challenging by modern readers, Leviticus provides the foundational understanding of holiness, atonement, and covenant relationship that underlies all biblical theology. Its twenty-seven chapters contain the sacred architecture of Israel's religious life and the moral framework for a society dedicated to serving the holy God.

Introduction and Structure

The Book of Leviticus derives its English name from the Greek translation "Levitikon," referring to the Levitical priests who were responsible for implementing its detailed instructions. The Hebrew title "Vayikra" (meaning "And He called") reflects the opening words of the text, emphasizing that these are not human innovations but divine revelations called forth by God Himself.

Structurally, Leviticus divides into two major sections: the ritual laws (chapters 1-16) and the holiness code (chapters 17-27). The first section focuses on the mechanics of worship, sacrifice, and priestly duties, while the second section addresses the broader implications of holiness for individual and community life. This organization moves from the specific requirements of formal worship to the comprehensive lifestyle demanded by covenant relationship with the holy God.

The book's setting remains fixed at Mount Sinai, where Israel received the law and constructed the tabernacle. The temporal framework spans approximately one month, from the completion of the tabernacle to the census preparations recorded in Numbers. This concentrated timeframe emphasizes the intensity and importance of the divine instructions being given to the newly formed nation.

Central to Leviticus is the concept of holiness, which appears in various forms over 150 times throughout the book. The repeated command "You shall be holy, for I the Lord your God am holy" establishes the fundamental principle that covenant relationship requires moral and ritual conformity to divine character. This holiness encompasses both separation from defilement and dedication to divine service.

The priestly perspective of Leviticus reflects its origin and purpose as a manual for religious leaders who would implement its instructions. The detailed procedural descriptions, precise measurements, and technical terminology demonstrate that this book was designed for practical use rather than abstract theological reflection. Yet these practical instructions carry profound theological implications about the nature of God, sin, and redemption.

The Sacrificial System

The Burnt Offering (Leviticus 1)

The burnt offering, or "olah" in Hebrew (meaning "that which goes up"), represents the foundational sacrifice of the Levitical system. This offering demonstrates complete dedication to God through the total consumption of the sacrifice by fire. The ascending smoke symbolizes the worshiper's prayers and devotion rising to heaven, while the complete burning signifies unreserved commitment to divine service.

The three categories of animals acceptable for burnt offerings—cattle, sheep and goats, and birds—accommodate different economic circumstances while maintaining the essential principles of the sacrifice. The requirement that cattle and sheep be male and without blemish establishes the principle that offerings to God must represent the best of what the worshiper possesses, not inferior or defective goods.

The ritual procedure begins with the worshiper laying hands on the animal's head, symbolically transferring ownership and identification to the sacrifice. This act of laying on hands creates a substitutionary relationship where the animal dies in place of the worshiper, bearing the consequences that would otherwise fall on the offerer. The subsequent slaughter by the worshiper emphasizes personal responsibility for sin and its consequences.

The priestly duties in the burnt offering involve receiving the blood, sprinkling it around the altar, and arranging the pieces on the altar fire. The careful attention to blood manipulation reflects its crucial role in atonement, as "the life of the flesh is in the blood." The priest's role as mediator between worshiper and God establishes the necessity of proper representation in approaching divine holiness.

The provision for bird offerings demonstrates divine concern for the poor who cannot afford larger animals. The procedure differs slightly—the bird is wrung by the neck rather than slaughtered, and the blood is drained at the side of the altar—but the essential elements of substitution and atonement remain intact. This accommodation ensures that economic circumstances do not prevent anyone from participating in covenant worship.

Theological Significance: The burnt offering represents the voluntary expression of worship, devotion, and complete surrender to God. Unlike sin offerings that address specific transgressions, burnt offerings demonstrate ongoing relationship and commitment to covenant faithfulness. The daily morning and evening burnt offerings established perpetual worship at the tabernacle.

The Grain Offering (Leviticus 2)

The grain offering, or "minchah" in Hebrew, represents the offering of human labor and agricultural produce to acknowledge God as the source of all provision. This non-animal sacrifice consists of fine flour, oil, and frankincense, with salt as a required seasoning and honey and leaven as prohibited ingredients. The grain offering often accompanies animal sacrifices but can also stand alone as an expression of thanksgiving and dedication.

The three forms of grain offering—raw flour, baked cakes, and cooked offerings from the griddle or pan—provide options for different circumstances and preferences while maintaining essential symbolic meanings. The fine flour represents the best of human agricultural effort, while the oil symbolizes richness and the Spirit's anointing. Frankincense adds the element of fragrant worship ascending to God.

The prohibition of leaven and honey in grain offerings reflects their association with fermentation and corruption, which are incompatible with offerings to the holy God. Leaven, in particular, becomes a consistent biblical symbol for sin and corruption that must be excluded from sacred contexts. The required salt, conversely, represents preservation and the enduring nature of covenant relationship.

The priest's portion of the grain offering—a handful burned on the altar as a memorial portion, while the remainder becomes food for the priests—establishes the principle of divine provision for those who serve at the sanctuary. This arrangement ensures that priestly dedication to religious duties does not result in material deprivation, while maintaining the sacred nature of offerings made to God.

The grain offering's emphasis on human production and labor acknowledges that worship involves not only blood sacrifice but also the dedication of daily work and material resources to divine service. This offering recognizes God's sovereignty over all aspects of life, including agricultural productivity and economic blessing.

The Peace Offering (Leviticus 3)

The peace offering, or "shelamim" in Hebrew (related to "shalom," meaning peace or wholeness), represents fellowship and communion between the worshiper and God. Unlike burnt offerings that are entirely consumed or grain offerings that become priestly food, peace offerings are shared between altar, priest, and worshiper, symbolizing restored relationship and communal celebration in God's presence.

The animals acceptable for peace offerings include cattle, sheep, and goats of either gender, reflecting the celebratory rather than atoning nature of this sacrifice. The requirement that animals be without blemish maintains the standard of offering the best to God, while the broader selection acknowledges the joyful context of thanksgiving and fellowship that characterizes peace offerings.

The ritual procedure follows the pattern established in burnt offerings—laying on of hands, slaughter by the worshiper, and priestly manipulation of blood around the altar. However, the disposition of the meat differs significantly, with specified portions burned on the altar, designated portions given to the priest, and the remainder returned to the worshiper for a communal feast.

The fat portions burned on the altar—the fat covering the entrails, the kidneys with their fat, and the fat tail of sheep—represent the choicest parts offered to God. The prohibition against eating fat or blood establishes dietary restrictions that maintain the sacred nature of these substances while providing practical health benefits for the community.

The communal meal aspect of peace offerings creates opportunities for family and community celebration in a sacred context. These shared meals strengthen social bonds while acknowledging God as the ultimate host who provides both the sacrifice and the blessing being celebrated. The requirement that the meat be consumed within specified time limits prevents abuse and maintains the sacred nature of the offering.

The Sin Offering (Leviticus 4:1-5:13)

The sin offering, or "chattath" in Hebrew, addresses unintentional sins and ritual impurities that disrupt the worshiper's relationship with God. This offering recognizes that even inadvertent violations of divine law create barriers to fellowship that require specific atonement. The graduated scale of required animals based on the offender's position demonstrates that greater responsibility accompanies higher status.

The sin offering for the high priest requires a bull and involves bringing blood into the holy place to sprinkle before the veil and apply to the horns of the incense altar. This elaborate ritual reflects the high priest's representative role for the entire community—his sin affects everyone, requiring more extensive atonement procedures. The burning of the entire carcass outside the camp symbolizes the complete removal of sin from the community.

The sin offering for the whole congregation, also requiring a bull, acknowledges corporate responsibility for sins that affect the entire community. The elders laying hands on the bull represents the community's ownership of both the sin and its atonement. The identical ritual procedure to the high priest's offering demonstrates that community sins are as serious as leadership failures.

The sin offering for rulers requires a male goat, reflecting their significant but lesser responsibility compared to priests. The blood remains in the outer court, applied to the horns of the altar of burnt offering rather than the incense altar. The priest receives the meat for food, establishing different levels of sanctity based on where the blood is applied.

The sin offering for common people requires a female goat or lamb, with provisions for birds or grain offerings for those too poor to afford animals. This accommodation ensures that economic circumstances do not prevent anyone from obtaining necessary atonement. The democratic availability of forgiveness reflects divine concern for social justice within the covenant community.

Key Principle: The sin offering system establishes that unintentional sins require intentional atonement. Ignorance of divine law does not eliminate the consequences of violating it, but God provides means for restoration when violations are recognized and properly addressed.

The Guilt Offering (Leviticus 5:14-6:7)

The guilt offering, or "asham" in Hebrew, addresses specific offenses involving sacred property or social relationships that require both divine forgiveness and human restitution. This offering recognizes that certain sins create debts that must be repaid in addition to receiving divine atonement. The combination of sacrifice and restitution demonstrates that true repentance involves making right both vertical and horizontal relationships.

Offenses requiring guilt offerings include inadvertent use of sacred property, uncertain ritual violations, false oaths, and social injustices involving deception or theft. The common element is the presence of measurable damage or definable obligation that requires specific compensation beyond the sacrificial atonement.

The restitution requirements involve paying the principal amount plus a twenty percent penalty to the injured party, whether God (through the priest) or another person. This additional payment acknowledges the harm caused by the offense and provides incentive for careful attention to obligations involving sacred property and social relationships.

The sacrificial animal for guilt offerings is consistently a ram, reflecting the serious nature of offenses that damage relationship and property. The uniform requirement regardless of the offender's social status demonstrates that guilt offerings address objective wrongs rather than matters of ceremonial status or social position.

The guilt offering's emphasis on restitution establishes the principle that forgiveness includes practical restoration of damaged relationships. Divine mercy does not eliminate the consequences of human actions but provides the framework for rebuilding trust and community harmony through appropriate compensation and sacrificial atonement.

Priestly Regulations and Duties

Instructions for Priests (Leviticus 6:8-7:38)

The detailed instructions for priestly conduct in handling various offerings demonstrate the sacred nature of their mediatorial role and the precision required in divine service. These regulations ensure proper execution of sacrificial rituals while maintaining the holiness necessary for approaching God on behalf of the people. The priest's role as intermediary between holy God and sinful humanity requires careful attention to divine specifications.

The perpetual fire on the altar represents continuous divine presence and availability for worship. The priest's responsibility to maintain this fire through daily attention to fuel and ash removal establishes the ongoing nature of priestly service. The special garments worn during ash removal and the clean garments for carrying ashes outside the camp demonstrate appropriate reverence for even routine duties.

The daily grain offering prepared by the priest from his own resources demonstrates personal investment in worship alongside official duties. The requirement that this offering be entirely burned rather than partially consumed shows that priestly service involves personal sacrifice as well as ministerial function. The anointed priest's dedication offering follows the same pattern, establishing continuity in priestly commitment.

The regulations regarding sin offerings consumed by priests establish both their provision and their participation in bearing the people's sins. The locations where different categories of sin offerings may be eaten—either in the holy place or not at all—reflect varying degrees of sanctity based on where the blood is applied. These distinctions maintain proper reverence while providing necessary sustenance.

The peace offering regulations specify the portions belonging to the priest—the breast presented as a wave offering and the right thigh as a contribution—while establishing time limits for consumption that prevent abuse and maintain sanctity. The prohibition against eating offerings with ritual impurity protects both the offerer and the priest from defilement.

The concluding summary of sacrificial regulations emphasizes that these are divine commands given at Mount Sinai, not human innovations or cultural adaptations. This divine origin establishes the authority and permanence of the sacrificial system while distinguishing Israel's worship from surrounding pagan practices.

The Consecration of Priests (Leviticus 8-9)

The ordination ceremony for Aaron and his sons represents the formal establishment of the Aaronic priesthood and demonstrates the elaborate ritual required for approaching divine holiness in official capacity. The eight-day ceremony establishes both the authority and the responsibilities of those who will mediate between God and His people. The public nature of this ceremony demonstrates divine approval and community recognition of priestly authority.

The ritual washing signifies purification from ordinary life and preparation for sacred service. The special priestly garments—including the breastplate with twelve stones representing the tribes, the ephod with its gold and colorful threads, and the turban with its golden plate declaring "Holy to the Lord"—establish both the dignity and the representative nature of priestly service.

The threefold anointing—of the tabernacle and its furnishings, of Aaron as high priest, and of the altar—consecrates both places and persons for divine service. The holy anointing oil, prepared according to divine specifications, symbolizes the Holy Spirit's enabling power for sacred duties. The prohibition against duplicating this oil for common use maintains its sacred character.

The sacrificial sequence during ordination—sin offering, burnt offering, and ordination offering—follows the pattern of atonement, dedication, and special consecration that will characterize ongoing priestly service. The application of blood to Aaron's right ear, thumb, and toe symbolizes the dedication of hearing, service, and walk to divine purposes.

The seven-day confinement at the tabernacle entrance emphasizes the transition from ordinary to sacred status and provides time for instruction in priestly duties. The warning that departure during this period would result in death demonstrates the seriousness of ordination and the life-and-death responsibility of priestly service.

Aaron's first official acts as high priest—offering sacrifices for himself and the people—establish the pattern of ongoing priestly service. The divine fire that consumes the offerings demonstrates God's acceptance of both the priesthood and the sacrificial system. The people's response of shouting and falling on their faces acknowledges divine approval and establishes proper reverence for priestly authority.

The Tragedy of Nadab and Abihu (Leviticus 10)

The immediate judgment on Aaron's sons Nadab and Abihu for offering "unauthorized fire" demonstrates the absolute necessity of following divine instructions precisely in matters of worship. Their violation—whether using wrong fire, wrong incense, wrong timing, or wrong authority—results in instant death by divine fire. This severe judgment establishes that familiarity with sacred things does not excuse careless or presumptuous behavior.

The prohibition against mourning for the executed priests and the command for Aaron and his remaining sons to continue their duties demonstrates that divine justice supersedes family loyalty and personal grief. The continuation of worship despite personal tragedy establishes the priority of covenant obligations over individual emotional responses.

The dietary restrictions for priests while on duty—prohibiting wine and strong drink—ensure clear judgment and proper execution of sacred responsibilities. The distinction between holy and common, clean and unclean, requires mental clarity and spiritual sensitivity that alcoholic beverages would compromise. These restrictions apply only during active service, acknowledging both human needs and professional requirements.

The dispute over the consumption of the sin offering reveals the complexity of applying ritual laws in unusual circumstances. Moses' initial anger at the apparent violation and Aaron's explanation about the inappropriateness of eating in light of the day's tragedy demonstrate the need for wisdom in applying legal principles to specific situations.

The resolution of this dispute through Moses' acceptance of Aaron's reasoning establishes the principle that ritual law serves spiritual purpose rather than mere external compliance. The high priest's judgment about appropriate responses to extraordinary circumstances receives divine validation through Moses' approval.

Laws of Ritual Purity

Clean and Unclean Animals (Leviticus 11)

The dietary laws distinguishing clean and unclean animals establish fundamental categories that will govern Israel's eating habits and separate them from surrounding nations. These regulations serve multiple purposes: promoting health through prohibition of disease-carrying animals, creating distinct cultural identity, and establishing symbolic categories that reflect spiritual realities about holiness and defilement.

The criteria for clean land animals—having divided hoofs and chewing the cud—eliminate pork (hoofs but no cud), rabbits (cud but no hoofs), and various other animals common in ancient Near Eastern diets. These restrictions require Israelites to think deliberately about their food choices and distinguish themselves from neighboring peoples through daily dietary decisions.

The water creature regulations—requiring fins and scales—prohibit shellfish, eels, and various other seafood while permitting most fish. The prohibition of creatures that died naturally or were torn by wild animals addresses both health concerns and religious principles about proper slaughter and blood removal.

The bird regulations, presented as a list of prohibited species rather than general criteria, focus primarily on birds of prey and scavengers that feed on carrion. The underlying principle seems to be avoiding creatures associated with death and violence, though some classifications remain unclear due to uncertain identification of ancient Hebrew terms.

The insect regulations permit locusts, grasshoppers, and crickets while prohibiting all other insects. The permitted insects become important protein sources during periods of agricultural difficulty, while the prohibited insects often carry disease or create agricultural damage.

The purification requirements for contact with carcasses establish degrees of ritual defilement and prescribed cleansing procedures. The distinction between animals whose carcasses cause defilement and those that do not reflects categories of holiness that extend beyond dietary regulations to encompass broader concepts of ritual purity.

Deeper Significance: The dietary laws create a comprehensive system of daily reminders about holiness, separation, and covenant identity. Every meal becomes an opportunity to remember covenant obligations and practice self-discipline in service to divine commands. These laws also promote community cohesion by creating shared practices that distinguish Israel from other nations.

Purification After Childbirth (Leviticus 12)

The purification laws following childbirth address the ritual impurity associated with the life-giving process and establish procedures for restoration to full participation in community worship. These regulations acknowledge both the blessing of new life and the reality of living in a world affected by sin and death. The prescribed offerings and waiting periods create space for both physical recovery and spiritual renewal.

The distinction between male and female children—seven days of impurity and thirty-three days of purification for boys, versus fourteen days of impurity and sixty-six days of purification for girls—has generated extensive discussion among interpreters. Some suggest this reflects ancient views of female physiology, while others propose symbolic or practical explanations related to circumcision timing and family dynamics.

The required offerings at the completion of purification—a burnt offering and a sin offering—acknowledge both thanksgiving for safe delivery and atonement for ritual impurity. The provision for a bird instead of a lamb for those who cannot afford the standard offering ensures that economic circumstances do not prevent proper purification and restoration to community worship.

The priest's role in declaring purification demonstrates the community aspect of ritual restoration. Individual compliance with purification requirements requires official recognition before full participation in worship can resume. This process maintains both personal responsibility and community standards for ritual purity.

Laws Concerning Skin Diseases (Leviticus 13-14)

The extensive regulations regarding skin diseases, traditionally translated as "leprosy" but encompassing various conditions, establish procedures for diagnosis, quarantine, and restoration that protect community health while providing hope for healing. These laws demonstrate divine concern for both individual welfare and public health, creating systematic approaches to managing infectious diseases in pre-medical societies.

The diagnostic procedures require priestly examination and re-examination to distinguish between temporary conditions and serious diseases requiring isolation. The detailed descriptions of symptoms—raw flesh, white hair, spreading lesions—provide specific criteria for consistent diagnosis. The requirement for professional evaluation prevents both unnecessary panic and dangerous neglect of serious conditions.

The isolation procedures protect the community from contagion while providing hope for eventual restoration. The requirement that infected persons identify themselves by calling "Unclean! Unclean!" creates necessary social distance while acknowledging the temporary nature of their condition. The dwelling outside the camp maintains community health without permanently excluding the afflicted.

The restoration procedures for healed individuals involve elaborate ritual ceremonies that acknowledge both divine healing and community reintegration. The complex offerings—two birds for initial purification, followed by extensive sacrifices after seven days—demonstrate the seriousness of restoration and the community's investment in welcoming back those who have been healed.

The extension of purity laws to clothing and houses demonstrates comprehensive concern for environmental factors that might harbor infection or contamination. The procedures for examining and treating affected materials establish principles of thorough investigation and appropriate response that protect community health through attention to material as well as personal cleanliness.

The Day of Atonement

The Sacred Ritual (Leviticus 16)

The Day of Atonement, or Yom Kippur, represents the culmination of the sacrificial system and the most solemn day in Israel's religious calendar. This annual ceremony addresses the accumulated sins of the nation and provides cleansing for the sanctuary itself, which has been defiled by the people's transgressions throughout the year. The elaborate ritual demonstrates both the seriousness of sin and the completeness of divine provision for atonement.

The high priest's preparation involves special bathing and simple linen garments rather than his usual ornate vestments. This humility emphasizes the gravity of the occasion and the priest's role as penitent representative rather than glorious leader. The personal sin offering for the high priest and his household demonstrates that even the mediator needs atonement before serving others.

The two goats selected for the people's atonement represent different aspects of sin's removal. The goat "for the Lord" is sacrificed as a sin offering, its blood providing atonement through death and substitution. The scapegoat, or "for Azazel," carries the people's sins into the wilderness, symbolizing complete removal and separation from the community. Together, these goats represent both the penalty of sin (death) and its elimination (removal).

The high priest's entry into the Most Holy Place represents the pinnacle of Old Testament worship and the closest human approach to divine presence. The cloud of incense protects him from the divine glory while the blood applications—on the mercy seat and before it—provide atonement for his own sins, the sanctuary's defilement, and the people's transgressions. This annual access foreshadows greater access to come.

The confession over the scapegoat involves laying both hands on its head while verbally acknowledging all Israel's sins, transgressions, and iniquities. This comprehensive confession addresses every category of wrongdoing while the physical gesture transfers guilt to the substitute bearer. The goat's release into the wilderness by an appointed man symbolizes the complete removal of sin from the community.

The concluding burnt offerings and additional purification procedures complete the day's rituals and restore normal worship patterns. The requirement that both the high priest and the man who released the scapegoat wash themselves and their clothes emphasizes the contaminating nature of contact with sin, even in the process of its removal.

The perpetual statute establishing the Day of Atonement as an annual observance creates ongoing provision for national cleansing and covenant renewal. The command to "afflict your souls" through fasting and refraining from work emphasizes personal participation in the community's repentance and creates space for serious reflection on sin's consequences and God's mercy.

Theological Significance: The Day of Atonement establishes the principle that sin's accumulated effects require comprehensive cleansing that addresses both guilt and defilement. The ritual's annual repetition acknowledges ongoing human sinfulness while providing reliable means of restoration. This ceremony becomes the theological foundation for understanding ultimate atonement through perfect sacrifice.

The Holiness Code

Regulations for Holy Living (Leviticus 17-20)

The second major section of Leviticus, often called the Holiness Code, transitions from ritual regulations to comprehensive lifestyle requirements that demonstrate what it means to be "holy as the Lord your God is holy." These laws address moral, social, and religious behaviors that distinguish Israel from surrounding nations and create a community worthy of divine presence in their midst.

The centralization of sacrifice at the tabernacle prevents unauthorized worship and maintains proper priestly oversight of offerings. The prohibition against sacrificing elsewhere eliminates competing religious centers and ensures that all worship meets divine standards. The death penalty for violations demonstrates the seriousness of maintaining proper worship protocols.

The prohibition against consuming blood establishes this substance as sacred to God and reserved for atonement purposes. The explanation that "the life of the flesh is in the blood" reveals the theological foundation for this restriction—blood represents life itself, which belongs to God alone. The requirement to cover the blood of hunted animals demonstrates respect for life even in permitted killing.

The sexual regulations in Leviticus 18 establish boundaries that protect family relationships and distinguish Israel from Canaanite practices. The comprehensive list of prohibited relationships—including various family members, multiple wives simultaneously, and same-sex relationships—creates clear moral boundaries while the concluding warnings about defilement and exile connect moral behavior with covenant blessing and judgment.

The diverse regulations in Leviticus 19 demonstrate that holiness encompasses all aspects of life, from religious observance to business ethics to treatment of neighbors. The command to "love your neighbor as yourself" provides the foundational principle that governs all social relationships and establishes the ethical framework for covenant community.

The penalties prescribed in Leviticus 20 for various moral and religious violations demonstrate the seriousness with which God views covenant obligations. The death penalty for adultery, incest, homosexual behavior, and child sacrifice reflects both the gravity of these offenses and the necessity of maintaining community purity. The graduated punishments for different offenses show divine justice while the calls for community participation in enforcement establish collective responsibility for maintaining moral standards.

Priestly Holiness and Sacred Times (Leviticus 21-25)

The additional restrictions placed on priests regarding marriage, mourning, and physical condition reflect their special role as representatives of divine holiness. The higher standards for priests demonstrate that greater privilege involves greater responsibility, while the absolute requirements for the high priest emphasize his unique function as the people's representative before God.

The regulations regarding sacrificial animals emphasize that offerings to God must be physically perfect, reflecting the spiritual perfection that characterizes divine nature. The minimum age requirements and specific disqualifications ensure that sacrifices represent the best of human resources rather than inferior or worthless goods.

The appointed feasts and sacred seasons establish rhythms of worship that structure Israel's year around recognition of divine provision and covenant relationship. The Sabbath provides weekly rest and worship, while the annual festivals commemorate historical deliverances and agricultural blessings that demonstrate ongoing divine faithfulness.

The Passover and Feast of Unleavened Bread commemorate Israel's deliverance from Egypt and establish perpetual remembrance of divine redemption. The Feast of Firstfruits acknowledges God's provision through agricultural blessing, while the Feast of Weeks celebrates the completed harvest and God's continued faithfulness.
